# Call Type Rubrics — Scoring Weight Adjustments

_Different call types have different success criteria. A discovery call shouldn't be scored against close-attempt criteria; a setter qualification call shouldn't be penalized for not closing the deal; a retention save call has totally different goals than any of the above. This reference maps call type to scoring weight adjustments._

## Why this matters

The 10 dimensions in `scoring-rubrics.md` are universal. Every call has rapport, discovery, objection handling, etc. But how much each dimension MATTERS depends on the call type.

A 3/10 close attempt on a discovery call is meaningless — discovery calls aren't supposed to close the deal. A 3/10 close attempt on a closer call is catastrophic — the close attempt is the entire point.

Always confirm the call type before scoring. Apply the weight table below to compute the overall grade.

## Call Type 1 — Discovery Call

### What good looks like

A discovery call's job is to uncover real pain, qualify fit, and earn the right to a deeper follow-up conversation (usually a closer call). The rep is trying to understand the prospect's world, not pitch them.

A great discovery call ends with the prospect saying *"this is exactly what I've been looking for"* — without the rep pitching. The framing of the offer is teased but not detailed.

### Dimensional weights for Discovery

| Dimension | Weight | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Rapport / Tone-Setting | 1.0× | Critical — bad opening kills discovery |
| Discovery Depth | 2.0× | The whole point of the call |
| Pain Agitation | 1.5× | Discovery without agitation is just data collection |
| Solution Framing | 0.5× | Light pitch only — should be teased, not detailed |
| Objection Handling | 1.0× | Some objections will surface, handle them but don't expect many |
| Close Attempt | N/A | Not the goal of this call type |
| Next-Step Clarity | 1.5× | The whole point — book the next call |
| Talk-Listen Ratio | 1.5× | Should be 30/70 toward prospect |
| Energy / Control | 1.0× | Lead the conversation but don't dominate |
| Compliance / Professionalism | 1.0× | Standard |

### Grade scale for Discovery

- A: Exceptional discovery, clear pain established, prospect leaning in, next-step locked
- B: Solid discovery, most pain established, next-step booked
- C: Surface discovery, some pain, next-step vague
- D: Weak discovery, no pain established, next-step unlikely
- F: Skipped discovery, pitched too early, no next-step

### What NOT to penalize

- No close attempt (not the goal)
- Solution presented lightly (correct for the call type)
- Rep talking less than prospect (correct talk-listen ratio)

### Common discovery call failures

1. **Pitching too early** — rep starts presenting the offer in minutes 5-10 instead of staying in discovery for 20-30 minutes
2. **Surface discovery** — accepts the first answer to every question instead of pulling deeper
3. **No quantification** — pain is named but never put in numbers
4. **No next-step** — call ends with "I'll send you some info"

## Call Type 2 — Setter Qualification Call

### What good looks like

A setter call is a short (5-15 minute) qualification conversation. The setter's job is to confirm fit (budget, authority, need, timeline — BANT) and book the closer. The setter does NOT pitch, does NOT close the deal, does NOT go deep on discovery.

A great setter call ends with the prospect booked into the closer's calendar with confirmed expectations for the next conversation.

### Dimensional weights for Setter

| Dimension | Weight | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Rapport / Tone-Setting | 0.7× | Brief — can't spend much time |
| Discovery Depth | 1.0× | Surface qualification only — BANT, not pain funnel |
| Pain Agitation | 0.3× | Not the setter's job |
| Solution Framing | 0.0× | Not the setter's job at all |
| Objection Handling | 1.5× | Setters get the most objections (price, time waste, "I'll think about it before talking to anyone") |
| Close Attempt | N/A | Not closing the deal |
| Next-Step Clarity | 3.0× | THE WHOLE POINT — get the closer call booked |
| Talk-Listen Ratio | 1.0× | Standard — slightly more rep talking is acceptable |
| Energy / Control | 1.5× | Setters need to maintain momentum or the prospect ghosts |
| Compliance / Professionalism | 1.0× | Standard |

### Grade scale for Setter

- A: Closer call booked at a specific time, prospect engaged, BANT confirmed
- B: Booked but soft on one BANT element
- C: Booked but qualification is shaky
- D: Soft commitment to follow up, no calendar booking
- F: No next step, no qualification, prospect lost

### What NOT to penalize

- No deep discovery (correct — that's the closer's job)
- No solution framing (correct)
- No close attempt (correct)
- Brief rapport (correct — time is short)

### Setter-specific scoring notes

The single most important thing on a setter call is whether the closer call was booked AT A SPECIFIC TIME on the calendar — not "I'll reach out next week" or "we'll connect later."

Common setter failures:
1. **Soft booking** — "let's connect next week" without a calendar invite
2. **Over-qualifying** — going too deep into pain, exhausting the prospect before the closer call
3. **Pitching** — starting to sell instead of just qualifying
4. **Not isolating BANT** — accepting "yeah I have budget" without confirming what budget actually means

## Call Type 3 — Closer Call

### What good looks like

The closer call's job is to win the deal. The rep walks the prospect through the offer in detail, handles objections, and closes. By the end of the call, the rep has either:
- A signed agreement
- A clear next step with a specific date (and a reason for the delay)
- A clean disqualification (this isn't a fit, both parties move on)

What's NOT acceptable: a closer call ending with "I'll think about it" and no follow-up scheduled.

### Dimensional weights for Closer

| Dimension | Weight | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Rapport / Tone-Setting | 0.7× | Should already exist from prior calls |
| Discovery Depth | 1.0× | Should re-anchor pain but not redo discovery |
| Pain Agitation | 1.5× | Critical — pain should be alive when offer is presented |
| Solution Framing | 2.0× | The pitch must be tied to discovered pain |
| Objection Handling | 2.0× | Most objections show up here, must handle well |
| Close Attempt | 2.5× | THE WHOLE POINT — must ask for the business |
| Next-Step Clarity | 1.5× | Either signed today or specific next step with date |
| Talk-Listen Ratio | 0.7× | More rep talking acceptable here (pitch + handling) |
| Energy / Control | 1.5× | Must drive momentum, can't let call drift |
| Compliance / Professionalism | 1.0× | Standard |

### Grade scale for Closer

- A: Deal closed in the call, or specific signed-by-X-date commitment
- B: Strong close attempt, real next step, deal still alive with specific path
- C: Soft close, vague next step, deal at risk
- D: No close attempt or close attempt fumbled badly, deal probably lost
- F: Disaster — wrong offer pitched, or rep collapsed at first objection, or no close attempt at all

### What to weight heavily

Closer calls are almost entirely about three things:
1. Did pain stay alive throughout the pitch?
2. Were objections handled with evidence?
3. Was there an actual close attempt?

Everything else matters less. A closer call with weak rapport but strong handling and a closed deal is an A. A closer call with great rapport, perfect discovery, but no close attempt is a D.

### Closer-specific scoring notes

Watch for:
- **The first close attempt** — when did it come? Was it after pain re-anchoring or before?
- **Multiple closes** — did the rep close once, accept the first response, and stop? Or close, handle, close again?
- **Implicit closes** — "Soft close" language like "let me know what you think" doesn't count as a close attempt. Score those at 3-4/10 on close attempt.

## Call Type 4 — Renewal Call

### What good looks like

A renewal call is with an existing client whose contract is up for renewal. The rep's job is to:
1. Re-anchor the value the client received
2. Hear and address any concerns from the past period
3. Confirm renewal (and ideally upsell)

A great renewal call ends with the contract renewed, or with concrete next steps to address concerns before renewal.

### Dimensional weights for Renewal

| Dimension | Weight | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Rapport / Tone-Setting | 1.0× | Existing relationship — easier but should reference shared history |
| Discovery Depth | 1.5× | "What's working / what's not" requires real questions |
| Pain Agitation | 0.5× | Less needed — value is the focus, not pain |
| Solution Framing | 1.0× | If upselling, frame against current state |
| Objection Handling | 1.5× | Cancel risk, downgrade risk |
| Close Attempt | 2.0× | Must ask for the renewal explicitly |
| Next-Step Clarity | 1.5× | Either renewed or specific concerns being addressed by date |
| Talk-Listen Ratio | 1.0× | Balanced — listen to feedback, talk through concerns |
| Energy / Control | 1.0× | Standard |
| Compliance / Professionalism | 1.0× | Standard |

### Grade scale for Renewal

- A: Renewed (and ideally upsold), client engaged
- B: Renewed without upsell, some concerns surfaced and addressed
- C: Renewed but client is shaky, churn risk
- D: Renewal stalled, concerns unresolved
- F: Client decided to cancel during the call

### Renewal-specific scoring notes

The biggest risk on renewal calls is the rep treating it as a routine check-in instead of a real sales conversation. Many reps don't even ask for the renewal — they just assume it.

Watch for:
- **Did the rep explicitly ask?** "I'd like to get your renewal locked in for another year — sound good?"
- **Did the rep proactively surface concerns?** "What's been frustrating about working with us this year?"
- **Did the rep reference specific outcomes?** "When you started, you were doing X. Now you're doing Y. That's because of Z."

## Call Type 5 — Retention Save Call

### What good looks like

A retention save call is when a client has indicated they want to cancel, leave, or downgrade. The rep's job is to:
1. Hear them out without defensiveness
2. Diagnose the actual reason (often different from the stated reason)
3. Either save the relationship with a creative remedy OR exit gracefully

A great save call either retains the client (with a remedy that addresses the real issue) or ends with mutual respect and a possible re-engagement door.

### Dimensional weights for Retention

| Dimension | Weight | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Rapport / Tone-Setting | 1.5× | Must not start defensive |
| Discovery Depth | 2.5× | Diagnose the real reason — this is the whole game |
| Pain Agitation | 0.0× | Wrong move on a save call |
| Solution Framing | 1.5× | Tailored remedy specific to their issue |
| Objection Handling | 1.5× | "I've already decided" is the meta-objection |
| Close Attempt | 1.5× | Must ask for the save (or graceful exit) |
| Next-Step Clarity | 1.5× | Either staying with specific terms or exiting cleanly |
| Talk-Listen Ratio | 2.0× | Heavily prospect-talking — they need to vent |
| Energy / Control | 1.0× | Calm, not aggressive |
| Compliance / Professionalism | 1.5× | Higher stakes — bad save behavior turns into a public review |

### Grade scale for Retention

- A: Client retained with terms that work for both parties OR client exits and refers someone else
- B: Client retained with concession, but underlying issue still smolders
- C: Client retained but unhappy, churn risk persists
- D: Client exits frustrated
- F: Client exits AND publicly criticizes (review, social, etc.) — rep made it worse

### Retention-specific scoring notes

The most common save-call failure is the rep treating it like an objection-handling exercise. It's not. The client has already decided. The job is to:
1. Find out what actually broke
2. Decide if it's fixable
3. Fix it or part well

Watch for:
- **Defensive language** — "Well actually we did everything we said we would"
- **Trying to talk them out of leaving** — instead of understanding why
- **Heavy discounting as a bandaid** — solves the surface, not the cause

A great retention rep often LETS the client go when the fit is genuinely broken — and earns a referral or a re-engagement later. Trying too hard to save every account makes long-term retention worse.

## How to apply weights when computing the overall grade

For each dimension, multiply the score (1-10) by the weight. Sum the weighted scores. Divide by the sum of weights to get the weighted average.

Example for a Closer call:
- Rapport 7/10 × 0.7 = 4.9
- Discovery 6/10 × 1.0 = 6.0
- Pain 5/10 × 1.5 = 7.5
- Solution 8/10 × 2.0 = 16.0
- Objection 4/10 × 2.0 = 8.0
- Close 3/10 × 2.5 = 7.5
- Next-step 5/10 × 1.5 = 7.5
- Talk-listen 6/10 × 0.7 = 4.2
- Energy 7/10 × 1.5 = 10.5
- Compliance 9/10 × 1.0 = 9.0

Sum = 81.1
Weight sum = 14.4
Weighted average = 5.6 → C grade

That call had decent fundamentals but the weak close and weak objection handling dragged the grade because they're weighted heavily for closer calls. The same dimensional scores on a discovery call would weight differently and might come out as a B-.

## Call type detection from transcript

If the user didn't specify the call type, look for these signals:

**Discovery call signals:**
- Length 25-45 minutes
- Mostly questions and answers
- Light or no pricing discussion
- Talk-listen ratio favors prospect
- Ends with "next steps" planning

**Setter qualification signals:**
- Length 5-15 minutes
- Heavy on BANT-style questions
- No pitch
- Focus on booking another call

**Closer call signals:**
- Length 30-60 minutes
- Detailed offer presentation
- Pricing discussion
- Multiple objections raised
- Close attempt(s) at the end

**Renewal call signals:**
- References to past work together
- "When you signed up..."
- Discussion of contract/term
- Possibly upsell language

**Retention save signals:**
- Reference to cancellation request
- Apology or defensive opening from rep
- Heavy prospect frustration
- "I've decided to cancel" language

If signals are mixed or unclear, ask the user. Don't guess.

## What to do with mixed-type calls

Some calls bridge types — e.g., a "discovery" call where the rep tries to close, or a "closer" call where the rep realizes they need to do more discovery first. For these:

1. Identify the PRIMARY type (what was the call meant to be?)
2. Score against that primary type's weights
3. Note the bleed in the report: *"This was scheduled as a discovery call, but the rep attempted to close. Either the call type was wrong, or the rep over-stepped — worth discussing in the 1:1 whether the structure should change."*

## Edge case: Inbound vs Outbound

The framework above works for both inbound (prospect requested the call) and outbound (rep cold-reached the prospect) calls, but the scoring tolerance is different:

- **Inbound calls** — prospect is warm, expectations are higher, rapport should be easier, weak rapport scores lower
- **Outbound cold calls** — prospect is cold, rapport is harder, slight allowance for bumpier opening

If the user specifies inbound vs outbound, slightly adjust rapport scoring (±1 point either direction) but keep all other dimensions the same.
