# Role-Play Scenarios

_A bank of 5-minute role-play scripts indexed by the rep's biggest gap. The skill pulls and customizes one of these for the rep's next 1:1, based on what the call review surfaced as the highest-leverage thing to fix._

## How to use this reference

Each role-play scenario follows the same structure:

1. **Title** — what gap this targets
2. **When to use it** — the symptoms that make this the right drill
3. **Length** — typical run time (3-5 minutes)
4. **Setup** — the persona the manager plays, the situation
5. **The provocation** — what the manager says to start
6. **The rep's job** — specific behaviors being drilled
7. **Success criteria** — what good looks like
8. **Variations** — how to make the drill harder or easier
9. **Coaching points after** — what the manager should debrief

When generating a role-play in the output, take the matching scenario and **customize** it to the rep's actual call:
- Replace generic prospect details with the prospect's actual details
- Reference the specific moment in the call this drill addresses
- Use the actual offer/pricing context
- Match the persona to the actual prospect type (gym owner, med spa, etc.)

## Scenario 1: Price Objection Drill

### When to use it
- Rep apologized for the price ("I know it sounds like a lot")
- Rep went silent after price objection
- Rep immediately offered a discount without isolating
- Rep argued the price ("Actually it's a great value")
- Rep skipped over the objection

### Length
4 minutes

### Setup
Manager plays a [match the actual prospect type — gym owner, med spa owner, real estate agent, etc.] who has gone through discovery, agreed there's pain, heard the offer, and is now hearing the price for the first time.

### The provocation
Manager opens with: *"Yeah... [pause]... that's a lot. We're a small business. I don't know."*

The pause is intentional. The "I don't know" is intentional. The rep needs to respond to a vague, slightly negative price reaction.

### The rep's job
1. Don't apologize ("I know it sounds like a lot")
2. Don't go silent
3. Don't immediately offer a discount
4. Don't argue the price
5. Use ALIA: Acknowledge → Loop → Isolate → Address
6. The loop step is critical — get the manager-prospect to articulate what "a lot" means specifically
7. Tie the address back to a specific quantified pain point

### Success criteria
- Rep gets the prospect to articulate what "a lot" means in concrete terms
- Rep connects the price to a specific pain established in discovery
- Rep isolates: "If we figured this out, would there be anything else stopping you?"
- Rep moves toward close, not away from it
- Rep's tone stays confident, not defensive

### Variations
- **Easier**: Manager gives a clearer objection ("It's $1,500 and that's more than I budgeted")
- **Harder**: Manager goes silent for 6+ seconds — rep has to be willing to break the silence with the right move
- **Hardest**: Manager pushes back on the loop ("It just is. I don't want to break it down")

### Coaching points after
- Did the rep apologize? If yes, why? What was the trigger?
- Did the rep loop or just respond?
- How did the silence feel?
- What did the rep notice about the prospect's energy when the loop landed?

## Scenario 2: Discovery Deepening Drill

### When to use it
- Rep accepted surface answers and moved on
- Rep asked discovery questions but didn't follow up
- Pain was named but never quantified
- Rep moved to pitch before pain was established
- Discovery felt like a checklist, not a conversation

### Length
5 minutes

### Setup
Manager plays a prospect who's willing to answer questions but defaults to vague, generic answers. The prospect's pain is real, but they won't volunteer specifics — the rep has to pull.

### The provocation
Manager opens with: *"Yeah we just want more clients, you know? Just looking for some help with growth."*

Generic, vague, low-information. Most reps move on after this. The drill is to NOT move on.

### The rep's job
1. Stay in discovery for at least 4 minutes — no pitching, no offer references
2. Get specific quantification of pain ("how many leads per month? What's a client worth? What's the gap to where you want to be?")
3. Pull at least 3 layers of "why" or "tell me more" on the same topic
4. Get to emotional pain, not just operational
5. Resist the urge to relate ("yeah, I hear that all the time") — keep asking

### Success criteria
- After 4 minutes, the rep can articulate the prospect's pain in specific dollar terms
- The rep has uncovered at least one consequence ("if this keeps going, what happens?")
- The prospect is now engaged, talking expansively, leaning in
- The rep has NOT pitched yet

### Variations
- **Easier**: Manager-prospect is willing to give numbers when asked directly
- **Harder**: Manager-prospect resists quantification ("I don't really track that")
- **Hardest**: Manager-prospect tries to flip the script and ask the rep questions instead — rep has to politely redirect back

### Coaching points after
- How many follow-up questions did the rep ask on the same topic?
- Where did the rep stop pulling?
- What was the exact moment the prospect shifted from vague to specific?
- How did it feel to stay in discovery longer than usual?

## Scenario 3: Close-Asking Drill

### When to use it
- Rep didn't actually ask for the business
- Rep used soft close language ("Let me know what you think")
- Rep made the prospect drive the close
- Rep got a "yes" signal and didn't capitalize
- Rep ended the call with a vague follow-up

### Length
3 minutes

### Setup
Manager plays a prospect who has been through discovery, heard the offer, and is now sitting in slight ambivalence — leaning toward yes but not over the line. The setup picks up at the end of the closer call.

### The provocation
Manager opens with: *"Yeah... I mean... I think this could work for us... [pause]"*

This is the moment of truth. The prospect is leaning in but hasn't committed. Most reps fumble here — they wait for the prospect to close themselves, or they soft-close, or they keep talking instead of asking.

### The rep's job
1. Make a clean, direct close attempt within 30 seconds
2. The close has to be assumptive but not pushy: "Sounds like we're a fit. The next step is just getting your card on file — what's the best email for the agreement?"
3. If prospect responds with hesitation, ask once more: "What's giving you pause?"
4. Lock in a specific next step with date and time before ending the call
5. No "let me know what you think." No "I'll send you some info." Real close.

### Success criteria
- Rep made an explicit ask ("Want to move forward?")
- Rep proposed a specific next step ("get your card on file," "send the agreement")
- Rep handled hesitation by asking what's blocking, not by retreating
- Call ends with either a yes or a specific scheduled follow-up

### Variations
- **Easier**: Manager-prospect says yes after the first close attempt
- **Harder**: Manager-prospect says "let me think about it" — rep has to handle the stall
- **Hardest**: Manager-prospect raises a new objection at close — rep has to handle the objection AND re-close

### Coaching points after
- How did asking feel?
- Did the rep wait for the prospect to volunteer the close, or did the rep lead?
- What language did the rep use to ask? Was it confident or apologetic?
- If hesitation came up, did the rep retreat or did they isolate?

## Scenario 4: Trust-Building Drill

### When to use it
- Prospect raised "how do I know this works" and rep gave generic reassurance
- Rep cited irrelevant case studies
- Rep got defensive when trust was questioned
- Rep didn't probe what proof would actually move the prospect

### Length
4 minutes

### Setup
Manager plays a prospect who has been burned before by a similar service and is openly skeptical. The prospect needs proof, not promises.

### The provocation
Manager opens with: *"Look, no offense, but we worked with another agency last year and they took $5K off us and produced nothing. How do I know you're any different?"*

The provocation is specific (named past failure) and direct (open skepticism). Most reps get defensive or go generic. The drill is neither.

### The rep's job
1. Acknowledge first ("I'm sorry that happened — that's a real failure")
2. Loop into the past failure ("What went wrong specifically?") — let them tell the story
3. Don't bash the previous agency
4. Don't make generic differentiation claims ("we're different because we care")
5. After they've told the story, ask: "What would you need to see to feel confident this won't be the same?"
6. Provide the specific proof they request

### Success criteria
- Rep didn't get defensive
- Rep let the prospect tell the full story of the past failure
- Rep asked what specific proof would close them
- Rep provided that proof, tailored to their situation
- Trust is visibly higher at the end of the 4 minutes

### Variations
- **Easier**: Manager-prospect names exactly what proof they want
- **Harder**: Manager-prospect doesn't know what proof they want — rep has to suggest options
- **Hardest**: Manager-prospect's past failure is structurally similar to what your offer does — rep has to differentiate honestly

### Coaching points after
- How did the rep manage their own defensiveness?
- What did the rep do during the prospect's storytelling?
- What proof did the prospect ask for? Was it surprising?
- Did the rep promise more than they can deliver?

## Scenario 5: Authority / Decision-Maker Drill

### When to use it
- Rep got "I need to talk to my partner/spouse/team" and accepted it without isolating
- Rep didn't pre-handle the decision-maker question in discovery
- Rep didn't propose a 3-way call
- Rep accepted "I'll get back to you" without a specific date

### Length
4 minutes

### Setup
Manager plays a prospect who has gone through the full call and now needs to "talk to my partner" (could be business partner, spouse, team — whichever fits the actual call's prospect type).

### The provocation
Manager opens with: *"This sounds great. I just need to run it by my [partner/spouse/team]."*

### The rep's job
1. Don't accept it without exploring
2. Acknowledge: "Of course, makes sense"
3. Loop: "What's the conversation going to look like? What are they likely to push back on?"
4. Isolate: "If [partner] said 'I trust your call,' would you be ready to move forward today?"
5. Address: Either propose a 3-way call OR arm them with specific language to defend the decision OR (if isolation revealed they're the actual blocker) re-engage with their concerns
6. Lock in a specific next step with a date

### Success criteria
- Rep didn't just accept the deflection
- Rep explored what the conversation with the decision-maker would look like
- Rep got a specific scheduled follow-up (not "I'll let you know")
- If a 3-way call was proposed, a date was set

### Variations
- **Easier**: Manager-prospect is willing to schedule a 3-way call quickly
- **Harder**: Manager-prospect resists the 3-way call ("they're really busy")
- **Hardest**: After isolation, manager-prospect reveals they ARE the decision-maker but were stalling — rep has to pivot to re-engage on the actual blocker

### Coaching points after
- Did the rep accept the deflection or explore?
- What did the isolation reveal?
- How was the next step locked in?
- If the prospect was the actual blocker, did the rep recognize it?

## Scenario 6: Silence-After-Objection Drill

### When to use it
- Rep filled silence with rambling
- Rep apologized to break silence
- Rep changed the subject when silence got uncomfortable
- Rep talked over the prospect's processing time

### Length
3 minutes

### Setup
Manager plays a prospect who, after hearing pricing or a major part of the offer, goes silent and stays silent. The drill is the rep's ability to not fill silence.

### The provocation
Manager states the price clearly, then says: *"Hmm."*

Then silence. 8-10 seconds. Just silence.

### The rep's job
1. Don't fill the silence
2. Wait for the prospect to speak
3. Maintain a calm, neutral facial expression (visible on Zoom)
4. If the silence gets to 12+ seconds, ask one calibrated question — not a justification: "What's going through your head?"
5. Resist any urge to apologize, justify, or pitch further

### Success criteria
- Rep stayed silent for the full duration
- Rep didn't justify the price or apologize
- When silence broke (either prospect or rep with calibrated question), the conversation continued at a higher level of engagement
- Rep's tone after silence was confident, not relieved

### Variations
- **Easier**: Manager-prospect breaks silence at 6 seconds with an objection — rep handles it
- **Harder**: Manager-prospect stays silent for 15+ seconds — rep has to be the one to speak, with a calibrated question
- **Hardest**: Manager-prospect breaks silence with sarcasm or hostility — rep has to stay neutral

### Coaching points after
- How did the silence feel?
- What was the rep's instinct to do? Did they resist?
- What does it mean that the prospect went silent?
- How does staying silent change the prospect's perception of the rep?

## Scenario 7: Reframe Drill (Challenger pattern)

### When to use it
- Rep is selling solutions, not insight
- Rep agrees with everything the prospect says
- Rep doesn't push back on a wrong assumption
- Rep is too "yes-rep"

### Length
5 minutes

### Setup
Manager plays a prospect who has the WRONG diagnosis of their own problem. Specifically, they think the issue is X, but based on discovery, it's actually Y. The drill is the rep's ability to reframe.

### The provocation
Manager-prospect says: *"Look, our issue is just that we need more leads. If we get more leads, everything else takes care of itself. That's all we need from you — more leads."*

Based on prior discovery in the call (the manager will frame this), the rep knows the actual issue isn't lead volume — it's lead-to-appointment conversion. The drill is to reframe without making the prospect feel stupid.

### The rep's job
1. Don't just agree ("totally, more leads, that's what we do")
2. Don't bluntly contradict ("actually you're wrong")
3. Use the Challenger reframe: "I want to push back on that gently — based on what you've told me, I'm not sure more leads is actually the problem. Here's what I'm seeing..."
4. Bring the data from discovery to support the reframe
5. Earn the right to the reframe by showing you've listened
6. Move the prospect toward the actual issue without making them defensive

### Success criteria
- Rep reframed without offending
- Rep used data from discovery to support the reframe
- Prospect either accepted the reframe or productively pushed back
- The conversation moved to the actual issue

### Variations
- **Easier**: Manager-prospect is open to the reframe
- **Harder**: Manager-prospect resists ("no, we just need more leads, that's what we said")
- **Hardest**: Manager-prospect gets defensive — rep has to maintain the reframe without losing rapport

### Coaching points after
- How did the rep set up the reframe?
- What language did they use to disagree without offending?
- Did the data from discovery actually support the reframe, or was it forced?
- How did the prospect's energy shift?

## Scenario 8: Stall ("Let Me Think About It") Drill

### When to use it
- Rep accepted "let me think about it" without isolating
- Rep didn't follow up with a specific question
- Rep agreed to "follow up next week" without a specific date
- Deal went cold after the call

### Length
3 minutes

### Setup
Manager plays a prospect who, at the end of the closer call, has not raised any specific objections but is hesitant. Their only objection is "let me think about it."

### The provocation
Manager-prospect: *"Yeah, this all sounds good. Let me think about it and get back to you."*

### The rep's job
1. Don't accept it as final
2. Don't pressure ("what's there to think about?")
3. Acknowledge ("of course, important decision")
4. Loop: "Help me out — what's the part you want to think through? Because there might be something I haven't covered well, and I'd rather you have the right info now than think on the wrong info."
5. Isolate: "If you had clarity on [whatever they say], would you be ready to move forward?"
6. If they reveal a real concern: handle it
7. If they don't: lock in a specific scheduled follow-up ("Let's lock in 10 minutes on Thursday at 2pm to talk through it")

### Success criteria
- Rep didn't accept "let me think about it" as final
- Rep surfaced a real concern OR locked in a specific follow-up date and time
- The deal didn't go cold

### Variations
- **Easier**: Manager-prospect reveals a real concern when looped
- **Harder**: Manager-prospect insists "no really, just need to think"
- **Hardest**: Manager-prospect reveals the real concern is something the rep can't easily address

### Coaching points after
- Did the rep surface a real concern?
- What language broke through "I just need to think"?
- How was the follow-up locked in?
- What's the rep's plan if the prospect ghosts?

## Scenario 9: Multi-Decision-Maker Drill

### When to use it
- Rep didn't qualify decision-making process upfront
- Rep was surprised by an unmentioned stakeholder at close
- Rep tried to override the unseen decision-maker
- Rep didn't propose getting the other stakeholder on a call

### Length
5 minutes

### Setup
Manager plays a prospect who said in discovery they were the decision-maker, but at close reveals there's actually another stakeholder (CFO, partner, board, spouse) who has to approve.

### The provocation
At close, manager-prospect says: *"This sounds great. I just need to run it by my [other stakeholder] before I commit."*

### The rep's job
1. Don't get frustrated or call out the discovery miss directly
2. Acknowledge ("that makes sense for a decision this size")
3. Pivot to qualifying the actual decision process: "What's their typical involvement in decisions like this? What do they care about most?"
4. Propose a 3-way call OR arm the prospect with specific talking points
5. Get a date for the next conversation — either the 3-way call or the prospect's debrief with the stakeholder
6. Don't pretend the discovery wasn't off — for next time, the rep should pre-handle this question

### Success criteria
- Rep handled the surprise without losing the deal
- Rep got specific information about the other stakeholder
- Rep proposed a concrete next step (3-way call or arming the prospect)
- Rep noted internally to ask the decision-maker question earlier in future calls

### Variations
- **Easier**: Manager-prospect is open to a 3-way call
- **Harder**: Manager-prospect resists involving the stakeholder ("I'll handle it")
- **Hardest**: Manager-prospect reveals the stakeholder is actually unsupportive ("they're going to hate this")

### Coaching points after
- Why didn't this come up in discovery?
- What question would have surfaced this earlier?
- How did the rep handle the surprise?
- What's the plan now?

## Scenario 10: Self-Sufficiency Drill ("We're Already Doing It Ourselves")

### When to use it
- Rep accepted "we're handling it in-house" without exploring
- Rep tried to talk the prospect out of their in-house approach
- Rep didn't surface the in-house results
- Rep pivoted away instead of engaging the objection

### Length
4 minutes

### Setup
Manager plays a prospect who, after discovery, raises that they're already doing this in-house with mediocre results.

### The provocation
Manager-prospect: *"Yeah, we've been running our own ads for a while. We're doing okay. Not sure we need an outside agency."*

### The rep's job
1. Don't dismiss ("yeah but you're not as good as us")
2. Acknowledge ("makes sense, lots of owners try in-house first")
3. Loop into the actual results: "How's it been going? What's your CPL? How many qualified leads are you getting per month? What's the show-up rate?"
4. Listen carefully — the answers will reveal the gap
5. Don't bash the in-house effort — name the gap with respect
6. Reframe: "Sounds like you're getting some results. The gap I see is between [their numbers] and [industry benchmark or what the offer produces]. Is that gap interesting to you, or are you happy where you are?"
7. Let the prospect decide

### Success criteria
- Rep got specific in-house numbers
- Rep identified the actual gap respectfully
- Rep let the prospect decide if the gap matters to them
- Either the deal continues with new framing OR ends with mutual respect

### Variations
- **Easier**: Manager-prospect's in-house results are clearly poor
- **Harder**: Manager-prospect's in-house results are decent — rep has to find the gap that still matters
- **Hardest**: Manager-prospect's in-house results are strong — rep has to recognize this isn't a fit and exit gracefully

### Coaching points after
- Did the rep get specific numbers?
- How did the rep frame the gap?
- What would have happened if the rep had dismissed the in-house effort?
- How did this position the rep — as a peer or as a salesperson?

## How to customize a scenario for the rep's call

When the skill outputs a role-play, take the matching scenario and:

1. Replace generic "manager-prospect" with the actual prospect persona (gym owner, med spa, etc.)
2. Use the actual offer/pricing the rep was selling
3. Reference the specific moment in the call this drill addresses ("This drill targets the moment at 22:08 when [prospect] said 'wow that's a lot' and you apologized")
4. Adjust the difficulty level based on rep's experience (junior reps get easier variations)
5. Add specific success criteria tied to what the rep actually fumbled

The role-play should feel like it was written FOR this rep, not pulled from a generic playbook.

## How to handle reps who resist role-play

Some reps think role-play is uncomfortable or beneath them. The skill output should make role-play feel valuable, not punitive.

Frame it as:
- *"5 minutes of practice now saves a $5K deal next call"*
- *"Pros practice. The reason this came up in your call is that you haven't drilled it enough."*
- *"Run this 3 times this week. You'll be different by Friday."*

Don't apologize for the role-play. Don't make it optional. Reps who do the work get better. Reps who don't, don't.

## When the rep is good and needs a stretch drill

For A-grade calls, the role-play targets a stretch skill, not a remedial one. Examples of stretch drills:

- **Compression drill**: "Run the same call again, but cut 8 minutes off the length without losing quality"
- **Same-day close drill**: "The prospect agrees verbally — now get the contract signed within the next 30 minutes, on this same call"
- **Reframe drill**: "The prospect is doing fine in-house — now find a strategic gap they didn't see, without bashing what they're doing"
- **Difficult-prospect drill**: "Same call, but the prospect is hostile to agencies because of past experiences — keep the call from going off the rails"

Stretch drills keep good reps engaged and growing.
