# Call Frameworks Decoded

_The major sales frameworks reps use, what each one looks like in practice, and how to score against the framework if specified. Use this when the user has indicated their rep is running a specific methodology._

## How to use this reference

If the user specifies that the rep is running a specific framework, score the call against the framework's pattern, not against the default consultative-selling rubric. A NEPQ rep who never asks a "consequence question" should score lower on discovery in a way that maps to NEPQ's specific structure — not a generic "weak discovery."

If no framework is specified, default to the consultative-selling rubric in `scoring-rubrics.md`. Most modern B2B selling is some flavor of consultative — the framework just provides specific scaffolding.

When framework adherence is part of the scoring, mention it explicitly in the dimensional justification: *"Discovery: 6/10. Within the NEPQ framework, the rep asked situation questions but skipped the consequence questions, which is the depth driver of the model."*

## Sandler

### Origin and reputation

Created by David Sandler, popularized in the 1980s. Still one of the most widely-used consultative-selling frameworks. Strong on disqualification — Sandler reps are willing to walk away from bad-fit prospects.

### What it looks like in practice

**Up-front contracts** at the start of every call:
- Purpose ("Here's what I want to accomplish today")
- Agenda ("Here's how I'd like to use the time")
- Outcomes ("By the end of this, here's what we'll have decided")

The up-front contract is non-negotiable for Sandler reps. If a Sandler rep skips it, they've broken the framework.

**The pain funnel**:
1. Tell me more about it
2. Can you give me an example?
3. How long has this been happening?
4. What have you tried to do about it?
5. Did it work?
6. How much do you think this has cost you?
7. How do you feel about that?

The pain funnel is the depth-driver. A Sandler rep who only asks the first 1-2 questions has bombed discovery.

**Reverse selling** — when prospects push back, Sandler reps "reverse" — meaning they back off and let the prospect re-engage. Looks like: prospect says "I'm not sure if this is right for us," Sandler rep says "Okay, fair — sounds like maybe this isn't a fit for you. Want to wrap up?"

This is counterintuitive. It tests prospect commitment. Done well, it transfers control. Done poorly, it kills deals.

**Negative reverse**: pre-empting objections by raising them yourself. "Some people in your shoes would say this is too expensive. Where are you on that?"

### How to score Sandler calls

Look for:
- Up-front contract at the start (5-point rapport boost if present, 5-point penalty if missing)
- Pain funnel depth (count how many of the 7 questions were asked)
- Whether the rep "reversed" appropriately when push-back came
- Whether negative reverses were used to surface objections
- Whether disqualification language appeared (Sandler reps qualify hard)

Common Sandler failures:
- Skipping the up-front contract
- Stopping the pain funnel after question 2 or 3
- Reversing too early (prospect hadn't fully committed yet)
- Forgetting the contract for next steps at the end

### Example Sandler exchange

**Up-front contract:**
*"Hey, before we dive in — here's what I'd like to do with our 30 minutes. I want to understand your situation, and you'll learn what we do. By the end, we'll either know there's a fit and move forward, or know there's not and end the call. Either is fine. Sound good?"*

**Pain funnel:**
- *"Tell me about your current Facebook ad situation."*
- *"What's that costing you in real numbers?"*
- *"How long has this been the case?"*
- *"What have you tried to fix it?"*
- *"Why didn't it work?"*
- *"What does that cost you?"*
- *"How does that feel?"*

## NEPQ (New Economy Power Questions)

### Origin and reputation

Created by Jeremy Miner. Massively popular in the 2020s, especially in coaching/courses/agency sales. Built around a neutral, problem-focused tone that doesn't trigger "salesperson" defensiveness.

### What it looks like in practice

**Three question types**:

1. **Connection/Situation questions** — neutral, low-pressure, get the prospect talking
   - *"What got you thinking about [topic]?"*
   - *"What's been going on lately that made you reach out?"*

2. **Problem awareness questions** — get the prospect to articulate their problem
   - *"What's the biggest challenge you're facing with [thing]?"*
   - *"How long has this been happening?"*
   - *"What does that look like day-to-day?"*

3. **Consequence questions** — make the cost of inaction visible
   - *"If nothing changes, where does this take you in 12 months?"*
   - *"What happens if this just keeps going?"*
   - *"What's the worst-case if you don't fix this?"*

The consequence questions are the depth-driver — the equivalent of Sandler's pain funnel. A NEPQ rep who skips consequence questions has stayed surface.

**Solution awareness questions** before pitching:
- *"If there was a way to [solve their stated problem], would you want to know more?"*
- *"What would it mean for you if you could [achieve outcome]?"*

**Calibrated tone** — NEPQ reps speak with downward inflection, slowly, in a neutral curious tone. They don't sound "salesy." This is part of the methodology, not just a style preference.

**The transition into pitch** is gentle — *"Based on what you've shared, here's what I think might fit..."* — not high-pressure.

### How to score NEPQ calls

Look for:
- Connection questions in the opening (no hard pitch)
- Problem awareness questions that go multi-layer
- Consequence questions specifically (this is where most NEPQ reps fail)
- Solution awareness questions before any pitch
- Calibrated, neutral tone (you can sometimes tell from the transcript by short, paced rep contributions)
- The transition into pitch (gentle vs jarring)

Common NEPQ failures:
- Skipping consequence questions — staying in problem awareness
- Pitching before solution awareness questions
- Reverting to traditional selling tone when objections come up
- Going too neutral and losing energy

### Example NEPQ exchange

**Connection:** *"What got you thinking about getting some help with marketing?"*

**Problem awareness:**
- *"How's your current Facebook ad performance?"*
- *"What's been the toughest part?"*
- *"How long's that been going on?"*

**Consequence questions:**
- *"If nothing changes, where does this leave you in six months?"*
- *"What happens if this keeps going?"*

**Solution awareness:**
- *"If there was a way to get qualified leads consistently without you running ads yourself, would you want to know more?"*

**Transition into pitch (gentle):**
- *"Based on what you've shared, I think there's a way we could help. Want me to walk you through what that might look like?"*

## Straight Line (Belfort)

### Origin and reputation

Created by Jordan Belfort. High-energy, urgency-driven, scripted. Effective for transactional B2C sales (financial products, real estate, time-sensitive offers). Less effective for high-ticket consultative B2B unless modified.

### What it looks like in practice

**Tonal control** — Belfort emphasizes specific tonal patterns:
- Certainty in the rep's voice
- Forced compression (talk fast, no air)
- Energy modulation (build up to the close)

**Three core elements** the rep must establish:
1. **You have the right product** (logical certainty)
2. **You can be trusted** (emotional certainty)
3. **The company can be trusted** (organizational certainty)

A Straight Line rep is constantly building these three certainties throughout the call.

**The Straight Line itself** — the rep keeps the prospect on a single, direct line from "interested" to "yes." Tangents, doubts, distractions are redirected back to the Line. This is more aggressive than Sandler/NEPQ — the rep takes more control.

**Looping** — when an objection surfaces, the rep doesn't try to argue it. They loop:
- Lower their tone
- Acknowledge ("I get that")
- Pivot to a credibility-building point
- Re-escalate energy

**The close** — Straight Line reps close hard. Multiple closes, rebuttals to "no," and energy that pulls the prospect across the line.

### How to score Straight Line calls

Look for:
- Tonal control (you can sometimes detect from transcript: short rep contributions in early call, longer ones at close)
- The three certainties built throughout
- Whether the rep stayed on the Line or got pulled into tangents
- Looping when objections came up (pivot, not argue)
- Multiple close attempts
- Energy level matching the moment

Common Straight Line failures:
- Skipping certainty-building (jumping to close before trust is established)
- Letting prospect lead the conversation off the Line
- Looping that feels manipulative or scripted
- Closing too hard, too early

Note: For high-ticket B2B (anything $5K+), pure Straight Line often hurts more than helps. The high-pressure approach triggers buyer defensiveness. Score Straight Line on B2B calls more carefully — what's "good Straight Line" might be "bad B2B selling."

## Challenger Sale

### Origin and reputation

Created by Matthew Dixon and Brent Adamson, based on Corporate Executive Board research. Built for complex B2B sales, especially when the prospect's own thinking needs to be reshaped. The "challenger" rep teaches, tailors, and takes control.

### What it looks like in practice

**Teach** — the rep brings new insight the prospect didn't know. Not features, not pitch — actual education that reframes how the prospect sees their problem.

**Tailor** — the insight is specific to the prospect's situation, not generic.

**Take control** — the rep is willing to push back on the prospect, name uncomfortable truths, and challenge bad assumptions.

**The reframe** is the move:
- *"Most gym owners think the problem is their ads. The data we're seeing actually points to it being the follow-up. Here's what I mean..."*

The reframe makes the prospect think differently. Done well, it positions the rep as a trusted advisor. Done poorly, it sounds arrogant.

**Constructive tension** — Challenger reps create tension that drives action. They're willing to disagree with the prospect. They're willing to walk away from a deal that isn't right. Not a "yes-rep."

### How to score Challenger calls

Look for:
- Did the rep bring new insight (teach moment)?
- Was the insight tailored to the prospect's specific situation?
- Did the rep challenge any of the prospect's assumptions?
- Did the rep maintain control of the conversation?
- Was constructive tension present, or did the rep collapse to agreement?

Common Challenger failures:
- Teaching nothing new — just pitching
- Generic insight that any prospect would have heard
- Not challenging any prospect assumption
- Backing off when push-back came

## MEDDIC / MEDDPICC

### Origin and reputation

Created at PTC in the 1990s. Now standard at most enterprise SaaS companies. More of a qualification framework than a selling methodology — used to score deal health and forecast accurately.

### What it looks like in practice

**M**etrics — what does the prospect measure success by? Specific numbers.

**E**conomic Buyer — who actually has budget authority? Have we met them?

**D**ecision Criteria — what specifically is being evaluated? What's required to win this deal?

**D**ecision Process — what are the steps to a yes? Who approves? In what order?

**P**aper Process — what does procurement/legal review look like?

**I**dentify Pain — what specifically is the prospect trying to solve?

**C**hampion — who internally is selling for us when we're not in the room?

**C**ompetition — who else is being evaluated?

A MEDDIC-trained rep should leave a discovery call with all 7-8 of these answered. If they didn't, the deal is at risk.

### How to score MEDDIC calls

Score against how many of the elements were uncovered:

- 7-8/8: A-grade qualification
- 5-6/8: B-grade — some gaps but workable
- 3-4/8: C-grade — significant blind spots
- 0-2/8: F-grade — deal is essentially unqualified

For each missing element, name the specific question that would have surfaced it.

### Common MEDDIC failures

- Skipping Economic Buyer (the rep talked to a champion and assumed they were the EB)
- Skipping Paper Process (deal stalls in legal review the rep didn't see coming)
- Identifying Pain shallow (named pain but didn't quantify)
- No Champion identified (deal has no internal advocate)

## Custom / Internal Frameworks

### When the user provides their own framework

If the user pastes or describes their internal framework:

1. Read it carefully
2. Identify the key stages or elements
3. Score against those stages, not the default rubric
4. Use the framework's terminology in your justifications

### How to handle a user-provided framework

Ask: "Do you want me to score against this framework specifically, or use it as a supplement to the standard consultative rubric?"

If the user wants framework-only scoring, ignore the standard rubric and build the report around their framework.

If the user wants both, run the standard 10-dimension scoring AND add a framework-adherence section that scores how well the rep ran their framework.

## What to do when no framework is specified

Default to the consultative-selling rubric in `scoring-rubrics.md`. Most modern B2B selling is some flavor of consultative — the rep is qualifying, uncovering pain, framing solutions, handling objections, and closing. The 10 dimensions cover all of this without requiring a specific framework.

If you suspect the rep is running a specific framework based on the transcript (uses NEPQ-style consequence questions, or Sandler-style up-front contracts), you can mention it: *"This call shows NEPQ-style structure — was the rep trained in that framework?"* But don't assume. Ask if it matters for scoring.

## How to mention framework adherence in the report

When framework is specified, add a section to the executive scorecard:

```
FRAMEWORK ADHERENCE: [NEPQ]

Hits:
- Connection questions in opening ✓
- Problem awareness depth ✓
- Solution awareness questions before pitch ✓

Misses:
- Consequence questions skipped ✗ (the depth-driver of the framework)
- Calibrated tone broke during objection handling ✗

Framework score: 6/10
```

This makes it clear what the rep is doing right and wrong specifically inside their training, not just in generic selling.

## A note on framework purity

In real calls, reps usually mix frameworks. A NEPQ rep might use a Sandler up-front contract. A Sandler rep might use a Challenger reframe. This is fine — frameworks are tools, not religions.

When scoring a call that mixes frameworks, score against the user's stated framework but acknowledge the mix. *"The rep ran NEPQ in discovery and shifted to Straight Line energy at close. The shift broke continuity — let's discuss whether that was intentional."*

## When the rep clearly doesn't have a framework

Some reps wing every call. The transcript will show:
- Inconsistent structure call-to-call
- No identifiable opening pattern
- Discovery questions that don't follow any logical progression
- Closes that range from soft to nonexistent

For these reps, scoring against any framework is unfair. Use the default consultative rubric and recommend the rep adopt a framework as a top coaching priority. Most reps benefit enormously from adopting any framework — the specific choice matters less than having one.
