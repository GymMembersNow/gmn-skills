# Coaching Language Patterns

_How to write feedback that actually changes behavior. The patterns below distinguish a useful coaching report from a useless one. Apply these to every section of the output — dimensional justifications, missed-moment analyses, rewritten responses, role-play scripts, CRM summaries._

## The core principle

**Feedback that doesn't change behavior is failed feedback.** A 2,000-word scorecard the rep reads, nods at, and then makes the same mistakes on next week is worse than no scorecard. Every coaching point in the output must clear the test: *would a thoughtful rep know specifically what to do differently next call?*

If the coaching point is *"work on your discovery"*, no — they don't know what to change.
If the coaching point is *"at 14:32 when she said 'we just want more leads,' you said 'great, more leads is what we do' — instead, ask 'how many is "more"? What does that look like in real numbers?'"* — yes, they know exactly what to do.

## Pattern 1: Anchor every point to a specific transcript moment

Vague: *"Discovery was weak."*
Specific: *"At 8:14, when the prospect said 'leads aren't great right now,' you moved on without quantifying. The next 6 minutes never came back to numbers, so when you got to pricing, the prospect had no concrete pain to weigh against the cost."*

Vague: *"Good rapport."*
Specific: *"At 0:47, you referenced their LinkedIn post about gym attrition — that was the move. Their tone shifted from guarded to engaged within 90 seconds, and they brought it up again at 12:30. Keep doing this."*

Every coaching observation needs a moment to point at. If you can't find the moment, you don't have a coaching point — you have a vibe.

## Pattern 2: Quote verbatim, don't paraphrase

When you reference what the rep said or what the prospect said, use the exact words. Quotation marks. Verbatim.

Wrong: *"You essentially told them you'd help, without specifying."*
Right: *"You said 'yeah we can definitely help you with that.' The word 'definitely' carried a promise without specifics — the prospect heard a guarantee."*

Reps can't argue with their own words. They can argue with paraphrasing. Quote them and the conversation shifts from "I disagree with your interpretation" to "I see what I said."

## Pattern 3: Cause-and-effect framing for criticism

Don't just identify what was missed — connect it to consequence.

Weak: *"You should have asked more discovery questions."*
Strong: *"Because discovery stopped at 'we want more clients,' when you got to pricing, the $1,500/month felt expensive to them — there was nothing concrete to weigh it against. If you'd quantified earlier ('what's a client worth to you?'), $1,500 would have been measured against a $4,000 LTV. Same price, different reaction."*

The cause-and-effect framing teaches the rep something — discovery isn't just "more questions," it's "the foundation that makes pricing land."

## Pattern 4: Alternative response framing

For every missed moment, show what could have happened. Not just "you missed this" but "here's what would have moved her forward."

Weak: *"You shouldn't have apologized for the price."*
Strong: *"At 22:08, when she said 'wow that's a lot,' you said 'I know it sounds like a lot, but...' That apology cost you. Try this instead: 'It is a real investment. And you mentioned earlier you're losing $4K/month on no-shows alone. Want to walk through how this addresses that specifically?'"*

The alternative response gives the rep a tool. They can practice it. They can use it next time.

## Pattern 5: Praise with the same specificity as criticism

Most coaching reports phone in the praise section. "Great rapport, good energy, professional tone." Useless. The rep doesn't know what specifically to keep doing.

Bad praise: *"Strong opening."*
Good praise: *"At 0:47, you opened with 'I saw on the form you mentioned you've tried Facebook ads before and got burned — I want to dig into that.' That's the move. You did three things in one sentence: you proved you read the form (trust), you signaled you'd actually listen (rapport), and you set the topic (control). Open every call this way."*

Praise that's as specific as criticism teaches the rep what their actual strengths are. Vague praise feels nice but doesn't help.

## Pattern 6: Respect the rep's intelligence

Tone matters. The same observation can be delivered as condescending or as collegial.

Condescending: *"You don't seem to understand that discovery comes before pitch. You started pitching at 4:30."*
Collegial: *"You started presenting the offer at 4:30 — earlier than ideal. By that point you'd asked two discovery questions. The prospect hadn't articulated pain yet, so the pitch landed on dry ground."*

The collegial version assumes the rep is capable and just made a misstep. The condescending version positions the rep as someone who doesn't understand basic concepts. The first one lands. The second one creates resistance.

## Pattern 7: One coaching point per moment

Don't pile on. If a single moment had three things wrong with it, pick the most important one and coach that. Mention the others briefly but don't make the rep feel like every breath was a mistake.

Pile-on: *"At 14:32 you went silent, then apologized, then changed the subject without addressing it, then started talking about features the prospect didn't ask about."*

One-point: *"At 14:32, after she said 'I've been burned by agencies before,' you went silent for 4 seconds and changed the subject. The silence + subject change told her you didn't have a real answer. Try this: 'I'm sorry that happened — what went wrong specifically?' That single question would have transferred her bad experience out of you and given you the actual differentiation moment."*

## Pattern 8: Frame the rep's voice in rewritten responses

When you rewrite a missed objection response, write it in the rep's actual voice — not in a generic sales-coach voice.

If the rep:
- Says "yeah" not "yes" — your rewrite uses "yeah"
- Doesn't use big words — your rewrite stays simple
- Has a specific catchphrase — incorporate it if natural
- Speaks slowly with pauses — keep the cadence

The rewrite should sound like the rep on a really good day. Not like a different person.

Wrong (generic coach voice):
*"I appreciate your concern about the investment. Let me reframe that for you. What's the cost of not solving this problem?"*

Right (matched to a casual rep's actual voice):
*"Yeah I hear you, the price is real. Quick thing though — earlier you said you're losing about $4K a month on no-shows. So we're really comparing $1,500 against $4K, right?"*

## Pattern 9: Make the role-play script playable

A role-play script that's vague isn't a drill — it's homework that doesn't get done. Make the script:

- **Concrete** — specific prospect persona with name, situation, pain points
- **Time-bound** — exactly how long it should take (3-5 minutes is the right range)
- **Outcome-defined** — what does success look like? "The rep handles the price objection without apologizing"
- **Repeatable** — the manager can run it multiple times with slight variations

Bad role-play script:
*"Practice handling the price objection."*

Good role-play script:
```
ROLE-PLAY: PRICE OBJECTION DRILL
Length: 4 minutes
Setup: Manager plays a gym owner who said yes to discovery, knows you have a 6-week 
challenge program, and is now hearing the price for the first time.

Manager opening line: "Yeah, $1,500/month. That's... that's a lot. We're a small gym."

Rep's job: Use ALIA. Acknowledge → Loop → Isolate → Address. Specifically:
1. Don't apologize for the price
2. Don't go silent
3. Don't immediately offer a discount
4. Loop the objection back to discovery findings — what did we already establish 
   that this price is being compared to?

Success criteria: Rep gets the prospect to articulate what "a lot" means specifically, 
and connects the price to a quantified pain point already discussed.
```

That's a real drill. The manager can run it. The rep can practice it. Repeatable.

## Pattern 10: The CRM summary discipline

The CRM-ready summary has to fit in one paragraph (~150 words max). Most reps and managers won't read more than that when they paste it into 1:1 notes. Compress hard.

Bad CRM summary (too long):
*"This was Iron Works Gym, a 12-month discovery call with [Owner Name], conducted by [Rep Name] on April 22nd. The rep opened well by referencing the prospect's form submission about Facebook ads, which built immediate rapport. However, discovery was weak — the rep moved through topics without going deep, and pain was named but not quantified. When pricing came up, the prospect raised the cost objection..."*

Good CRM summary (one tight paragraph):
*"Discovery call with Iron Works Gym, [Date]. [Rep] opened strong but discovery stayed surface — pain was named (no-shows, weak ad performance) but never quantified. Price objection at 22:08 was apologized for, not handled. Three coaching priorities: (1) quantify pain in dollars during discovery, (2) handle price objections by looping back to quantified pain, (3) drop apologetic language. Next 1:1 should drill price-objection handling using the role-play script. Overall: B-."*

That's tight, specific, and action-oriented. A manager can read it in 20 seconds and know exactly what to focus on.

## Pattern 11: Avoid the language failure modes

Specific phrases that signal weak coaching:

**Avoid:**
- "Could be better" — useless
- "Try to be more confident" — coaching by adjective, not behavior
- "Work on your discovery" — vague directive
- "You should ask more questions" — which questions?
- "It would have been better if..." — passive, no specific alternative
- "Just remember to..." — instructions without context
- "Always do X" / "Never do Y" — absolute rules ignore context

**Prefer:**
- "At [timestamp], here's what specifically would have moved her forward..."
- "When she said [exact quote], the response that works is [specific alternative]..."
- "You said [exact quote] — try [exact alternative] instead. The difference: [why]"
- "Good move at [timestamp] — you did [specific thing], which produced [specific result]. Keep doing this."

## Pattern 12: When to be direct vs. when to be gentle

Default to direct. Most reps benefit from clear, specific feedback delivered with respect. They don't need cushioning.

But there are cases where directness without context damages the coaching:

- **The rep is clearly junior** — soften slightly, frame as "this is what experienced reps do" rather than "you should know this"
- **The mistake had identity-level consequences** (rep was rude, broke trust with a prospect, cost a major deal) — be direct about the impact but not punitive about the person
- **The pattern shows up in every call** (chronic issue) — don't pretend it's a one-time thing, name the pattern explicitly: "This is the third call where you've apologized for the price. It's a pattern. Let's break it."

## Pattern 13: Identify the rep's biggest single gap

The role-play script targets ONE gap, not three. Most reps can only work on one thing at a time. Pick the one with the highest leverage and make the role-play hit it hard.

How to identify the biggest single gap:

1. Look at the dimensional scores — which one is lowest AND highest-weighted for this call type?
2. Look at the missed moments — which one cost the most (potentially the deal)?
3. Look at the pattern — does this same mistake show up multiple times in the call?

The intersection of those three is the biggest gap. The role-play targets that.

## Pattern 14: The CRM summary should imply the next conversation

A great CRM summary doesn't just describe the call — it sets up the next conversation. It points the manager toward what to discuss in the 1:1.

Without setup: *"Call was a B-. Discovery was weak."*
With setup: *"Call was a B-. Discovery was weak — specifically, pain wasn't quantified before pricing came up. Drill quantification questions in the next 1:1."*

The second version implies action. The first doesn't.

## Pattern 15: Distinguish skill issues from will issues

When evaluating, distinguish:

- **Skill issue** — the rep doesn't know how to do this thing yet (e.g., they didn't know to ask consequence questions)
- **Will issue** — the rep knows but isn't doing it (e.g., they apologized for the price even though they've been told 10 times not to)

Skill issues need teaching. Will issues need direct accountability.

You can sometimes tell from the transcript:
- If the rep half-tried something and got it slightly wrong, it's likely a skill issue
- If the rep skipped a thing entirely that they've been coached on before, it's likely a will issue

If the user provides context that this is the rep's third bad call on the same pattern, name it: *"This is the third call I'm reviewing where you apologized for the price. The drill isn't going to fix this — you know what to do. The question is whether you're going to do it. Let's talk about that in the 1:1."*

That's harder feedback but more honest. It respects the rep enough to be straight with them.

## Pattern 16: Ranking coaching priorities by IMPACT, not severity

The biggest mistake gets the most coverage in the report. The most coachable mistake gets ranked first in priorities.

These aren't always the same.

Example:
- Mistake A: Rep was 30 seconds late starting the call. Severity: medium (rude). Coachability: easy.
- Mistake B: Rep apologized for the price. Severity: high (cost the deal). Coachability: hard, requires drill.
- Mistake C: Rep didn't quantify pain in discovery. Severity: high (made the price land bad). Coachability: medium.

Severity-ranked priorities (wrong way): B, C, A
Impact-ranked priorities (right way): C, B, A

Why? Because fixing C (better discovery) makes B (price objections) much easier to handle. C is the leverage point. Always rank the priority that, if fixed, makes other things easier first.

## Pattern 17: When the call was excellent, coach UP

A common failure mode is reviewing an A-grade call and giving generic praise. That wastes the opportunity. Even a great call has a next level.

For an A-grade call:
- Identify what specifically the rep did well (with quotes and timestamps)
- Identify the next level — the thing that would make them an A+
- The role-play targets a stretch skill, not a remedial skill

Example for an A-grade call:
*"This call was excellent — the discovery was quantified, the close was clean. Top 1% of the calls I review. The next level: you can shorten the call by 8-10 minutes without losing quality. Watch the section from 22:30-30:00 — you re-asked questions you'd already gotten answers to. That's a confidence pattern. You don't need to circle back; trust your first pull. The role-play this week is the same close, but with the goal of doing it in 25 minutes total."*

That's coaching up. The rep gets recognition AND a stretch goal.

## Pattern 18: Avoid the savior framing

Don't position yourself (or your coaching) as the rep's savior. The rep made the call. The rep is the one who has to make the next call. The coaching is just a tool they can use.

Wrong: *"Here's what I would have done in that moment..."*
Right: *"Here's the response pattern that works in that moment..."*

The first one centers the coach. The second one centers the rep's growth. The second is what we want.

## Quick voice-check before finalizing

Before delivering a coaching report, read the missed-moments section out loud. Ask:

- Does this sound like advice from a peer who knows their craft, or like a teacher correcting a student?
- Would I be okay reading this if I were the rep?
- Is every observation specific enough that the rep knows what to do differently?
- Is the praise as specific as the criticism?
- Does the role-play actually drill the biggest gap, or is it generic?

If any answer is no, revise. The goal is a report that the rep reads, says "okay I see it, let's drill it," and walks into the next call different.
