# Objection Handling Playbook

_The most common B2B sales objections, what they actually mean underneath, why reps fumble them, and the patterns that work. Use this when scoring the objection-handling dimension and when writing rewritten responses._

## How to use this playbook

Every objection has three layers:
1. **The surface objection** — what the prospect said
2. **The real concern** — what's actually going on underneath
3. **The pattern** — the response pattern that addresses the real concern, not just the surface

When evaluating objection handling in a transcript, ask:
- Did the rep hear the surface or did they get to the real concern?
- Did they isolate (confirm this is the only objection) or just respond?
- Did they address with evidence, or just reassurance?
- Did they move forward after handling, or stall?

When writing rewritten responses, use the pattern below as scaffolding, then customize to the rep's actual voice from the transcript.

## The universal pattern: ALIA

Across all objections, the high-handling pattern follows ALIA:

- **A**cknowledge — validate the concern without agreeing it's a dealbreaker
- **L**oop — ask a question that gets the prospect to elaborate
- **I**solate — confirm this is the only thing holding them back
- **A**ddress — respond with evidence specific to their situation

The acknowledgment + loop is what most reps skip. They jump straight to addressing, which feels defensive. ALIA buys the rep time to actually understand what's going on.

## Objection 1: Price ("It's too expensive")

### What it actually means

Surface: "It's too expensive."

Real concern (one of these):
- "I don't see enough value to justify this price"
- "I haven't been agitated about my pain enough to feel the cost of inaction"
- "I'm comparing this to a cheaper alternative I could buy instead"
- "I genuinely can't afford it right now (cash flow problem)"
- "I want a discount because I always negotiate"
- "I'm using price as a stalling tactic because something else is wrong"

Each of these has a different fix. Reps who treat all price objections the same get crushed.

### Why reps fumble it

- They get defensive ("Well actually, when you compare us to...")
- They cave instantly ("Okay, I can do 20% off")
- They go silent (the worst)
- They argue the price ("It's actually a great deal")
- They skip past it ("Anyway, as I was saying about the features...")

### The pattern that works

```
Acknowledge:    "I hear you. Price is real."
Loop:           "Help me understand — when you say 'expensive,' 
                 expensive compared to what?"
Isolate:        "If we figured out the price piece, would there be 
                 anything else holding you back from moving forward?"
Address:        Respond based on what they said in the loop step.
```

The loop step is everything. "Expensive compared to what?" forces the prospect to articulate what they're really comparing this to. If they say "compared to my budget," that's a cash-flow objection — different fix. If they say "compared to [Competitor X]," that's a value objection — different fix. If they say "I don't know, just expensive," that's a discovery failure — go back and re-agitate.

### Example rewrite

Bad rep response: *"Okay, I understand. Let me see what I can do on the price."*

Rewritten: *"I hear you — price is real. Help me understand: when you say expensive, expensive compared to what? Because earlier you mentioned you're spending $3K a month on Facebook ads and getting nothing back. I want to make sure we're comparing this to the actual alternative, not just the sticker price."*

### The exception

When the prospect is genuinely cash-strapped (small business, just started, etc.), ALIA still works but the address step is different. You're not selling them on value — you're either offering a payment plan, a smaller scope, or qualifying them out. Don't try to talk a broke prospect into a price they can't afford. That's not a deal; that's a refund waiting to happen.

## Objection 2: Timing ("Not the right time")

### What it actually means

Surface: "It's not a good time right now."

Real concern (one of these):
- "I'm avoiding the discomfort of making a decision"
- "I'm hoping a problem solves itself if I wait"
- "I have a competing priority I'm dealing with first"
- "I want to think about it without saying 'I want to think about it'"
- "I genuinely have a hard external deadline (busy season, hiring freeze, etc.)"

Most "not the right time" objections are not actually about time. They're about avoidance.

### Why reps fumble it

- Accept it as final ("Okay, when's a better time?")
- Try to manufacture urgency that feels fake ("Well, prices go up next week...")
- Push back without isolating ("Why isn't it the right time?")

### The pattern that works

```
Acknowledge:    "Totally fair. Timing matters."
Loop:           "When you say it's not the right time — is it that 
                 you've got something specific going on that's 
                 competing for your attention, or is it more of a 
                 'this is a big decision' kind of pause?"
Isolate:        "If timing weren't an issue, would this be the 
                 right thing to do?"
Address:        If yes → "Then let's figure out the timing piece. 
                 What's the actual right time, and what makes 
                 then different from now?"
                If no → "Okay, that's good to know. What's actually 
                 missing for you?"
```

The isolate step in the timing objection is a gold-mining question. If the prospect says "yes, if timing weren't an issue I'd do it," they've just told you the deal is winnable — you just need to find the actual blocker. If they say "honestly, no," you've saved everyone time and uncovered the real objection.

### Example rewrite

Bad rep response: *"Okay, when do you think would be a better time?"*

Rewritten: *"Totally fair, timing matters. Quick question — if timing weren't an issue, would this be the right thing to do? Because if yes, I want to make sure we're solving for the actual timing piece, not using it as a stand-in for something else."*

## Objection 3: Authority ("I need to talk to my [partner/spouse/team]")

### What it actually means

Surface: "I need to check with my [decision-maker]."

Real concern (one of these):
- "I'm using this as a stall — I'm the actual decision-maker but I want to think"
- "There's genuinely another person involved and I don't want to commit without them"
- "I'm worried about how to defend this purchase to that person"
- "I'm trying to get the rep off the call without saying no"

### Why reps fumble it

- Accept it without isolating ("Sure, talk to them and let me know")
- Try to override the other decision-maker ("Well, you can make the call, right?")
- Don't pre-handle it before the close (asking too late)
- Don't get a specific timeline or scheduled follow-up

### The pattern that works

The best handling happens BEFORE the close, not after. During discovery:

```
Pre-handle:     "Just so we're aligned — when we get to the end of 
                 this conversation, if it makes sense, who would 
                 be involved in the decision?"
```

If they name another person:
```
Pre-handle:     "Got it. Should we get them on a call together, 
                 or are you the one who decides and runs it by them?"
```

If the objection comes up at close:
```
Acknowledge:    "Of course — important decision, makes sense to 
                 align with [partner]."
Loop:           "What's the conversation going to look like? What 
                 are they likely to push back on?"
Isolate:        "If [partner] said 'I trust your call,' would you 
                 be ready to move forward today?"
Address:        Schedule a 3-way call. Or arm the prospect with 
                specific language to defend the decision. Or 
                discover that the prospect themselves is the actual 
                blocker and re-engage on their concerns.
```

### Example rewrite

Bad rep response: *"Sure, sounds good. Let me know what they say."*

Rewritten: *"Of course, that makes sense. Quick thought — would it help if I jumped on a 15-minute call with both of you tomorrow? That way I can answer any questions they have directly, and you don't have to be the one explaining why this matters. What's [partner]'s schedule like tomorrow?"*

## Objection 4: Trust ("How do I know this will work?")

### What it actually means

Surface: "How do I know this works / how do I know I can trust you?"

Real concern (one of these):
- "I've been burned before and I'm scared"
- "I haven't seen enough proof"
- "I don't trust the category — the world is full of scammy [agencies/coaches/SaaS vendors]"
- "Something about you specifically isn't landing"

Trust objections are usually a positive signal — the prospect is engaged enough to want proof. The concern is real, and the rep's job is to provide actual evidence, not just reassurance.

### Why reps fumble it

- Reassure without evidence ("Trust me, this works")
- Cite vague stats ("Our clients see amazing results")
- Get defensive ("Are you saying you don't trust me?")
- Cite irrelevant case studies (a million-dollar SaaS case study to a $5K/month coaching prospect)

### The pattern that works

```
Acknowledge:    "Smart question. You should be skeptical."
Loop:           "What would you need to see to feel confident? 
                 A case study from someone in your situation? A 
                 conversation with a current client? A guarantee?"
Isolate:        "If we got you that, would you be ready?"
Address:        Provide the specific proof they asked for. Match 
                the case study to their vertical, situation, scale.
```

The "what would you need to see" is the move. Most reps assume what proof matters. The prospect tells you exactly what proof would close them — if you ask.

### Example rewrite

Bad rep response: *"We have hundreds of happy clients. I can send you some testimonials."*

Rewritten: *"Smart question — you should be skeptical. What kind of proof would actually move you? Like, would a case study from another gym in your size range help? Or would talking directly to a current client be more useful? I want to give you the right thing, not just the obvious thing."*

## Objection 5: Self-Sufficiency ("We're already doing it ourselves")

### What it actually means

Surface: "We can handle this in-house" or "We're already running our own ads / doing our own marketing."

Real concern (one of these):
- "I want to maintain control"
- "I don't see the gap between my efforts and your offering"
- "I've been burned by agencies/vendors and prefer doing it myself"
- "I'm using this as a stall"
- "I genuinely have an in-house person who is good"

### Why reps fumble it

- Try to talk the prospect out of their in-house solution ("Yeah but you're not as good as us")
- Pivot to a different angle without addressing the core ("Okay well let me tell you about this other thing")
- Accept it as final ("Okay, sounds like you're set")

### The pattern that works

```
Acknowledge:    "Makes sense. Lots of gym owners try to do this 
                 themselves first."
Loop:           "How's it been going? What kind of results are you 
                 seeing from doing it in-house?"
Isolate:        "If we found that there's a meaningful gap between 
                 what you're doing and what we could produce, would 
                 you be open to it?"
Address:        Use what they tell you in the loop. Be honest. If 
                they're crushing it in-house, congratulate them and 
                end the call. If there's a gap, the gap will surface.
```

The loop step almost always reveals the gap. Most "we're doing it ourselves" prospects are doing it badly but don't have anyone telling them so. Their answer to "how's it going" is rarely "amazing — we don't need you."

### Example rewrite

Bad rep response: *"Well, are you sure you're getting the best results? Most in-house teams aren't."*

Rewritten: *"Makes sense — a lot of owners want to keep that in-house. How's it been going? What kind of CPL and show-up rate are you getting from your current setup?"*

## Objection 6: Information Request ("Send me information / a proposal")

### What it actually means

Surface: "Just send me some information / a brochure / a proposal."

Real concern (one of these):
- "I want to get off this call without committing"
- "I need to see something written down to evaluate it"
- "I need it for my internal approval process (legitimate)"
- "I want to think about it without saying that"

A real proposal request is rare. Most "send me a proposal" requests are stalls. But some are real, and you have to distinguish.

### Why reps fumble it

- Accept the request and send a 47-page proposal that never gets read
- Don't isolate the real concern
- Don't get a date for the next conversation
- Send the proposal and never hear back

### The pattern that works

```
Acknowledge:    "Happy to send something over."
Loop:           "What would you want it to include? Like, what's the 
                 actual question you're trying to answer by reviewing 
                 a written version?"
Isolate:        "If the proposal answered all your questions, would 
                 you be ready to move forward?"
Address:        If yes → "Then let's just answer those questions live 
                right now. Faster for both of us. What's the first one?"
                If no → "Okay, what's the actual concern? Because I 
                want to answer it directly, not bury it in a PDF."
```

This pattern is a power move. It treats the proposal request as the symptom, not the cure. Most prospects don't actually want a proposal — they want answers. Give them the answers live.

### Example rewrite

Bad rep response: *"Sure, I'll get that over to you tomorrow. What's your email?"*

Rewritten: *"Yeah I can do that — quick thing first. What questions are you hoping to answer when you read it? Because if it's just a couple things, I can probably answer them right now and save us both time."*

## Objection 7: Stall ("Let me think about it")

### What it actually means

Surface: "Let me think it over."

Real concern (almost always):
- "I have an unspoken objection I haven't told you about"
- "I'm not ready to say no but I'm not convinced"

There is essentially never a real "I need to think about it" — that's a fiction. Either there's a specific concern the prospect didn't surface, or they need information they didn't ask for, or they're stalling to avoid saying no. The rep's job is to find which one.

### Why reps fumble it

- Accept it ("Sure, take your time")
- Pressure ("What's there to think about?")
- Don't follow up ("I'll check in next week" — never does)
- Get defensive

### The pattern that works

```
Acknowledge:    "Of course — important decision."
Loop:           "Help me out — what's the part you want to think 
                 through? Because there might be something I haven't 
                 covered well, and I'd rather you have the right 
                 info now than think on the wrong info."
Isolate:        "If you had the answer to [whatever they say], would 
                 you be ready to move forward? Or is there something 
                 else?"
Address:        Answer the actual concern. Or schedule a specific 
                follow-up call ("Let's lock in 10 minutes Thursday 
                at 2pm to talk through it" — not "I'll check in next 
                week").
```

### Example rewrite

Bad rep response: *"Sure, no problem. I'll follow up with you in a few days."*

Rewritten: *"Of course — important decision. Quick question: what's the part you want to think through? Because if there's something specific I haven't covered well, I'd rather you have the right info now than spend a week thinking on the wrong info. What's the piece that's giving you pause?"*

## Objection 8: Past Failure ("We tried something like this before and it didn't work")

### What it actually means

Surface: "We've tried this before and got burned" or "We worked with [Other Agency] and it was a disaster."

Real concern (always):
- "I'm scared of repeating the same mistake"
- "I need to know what's different about you specifically"

This is not really an objection — it's a request for differentiation. Reps who treat it as an objection get defensive. Reps who treat it as a request for clarity get the deal.

### Why reps fumble it

- Bash the previous vendor ("Yeah, [other agency] is terrible")
- Generic "we're different" claims
- Don't dig into what specifically went wrong before
- Don't connect their specific differentiator to the prospect's specific bad experience

### The pattern that works

```
Acknowledge:    "I'm sorry that happened. What went wrong specifically?"
Loop:           "Really listen. Don't interrupt. Let them tell the 
                 full story. The longer they talk, the more pain 
                 they're transferring out of [other vendor] and into 
                 a clean slate for you."
Isolate:        "If we could solve specifically [the thing that went 
                 wrong], would that change how you're thinking about 
                 this?"
Address:        Connect your differentiator to the specific failure 
                they described. Not generic differentiation — specific 
                to their wound.
```

### Example rewrite

Bad rep response: *"Yeah, a lot of agencies are bad. We're different because we actually care."*

Rewritten: *"I'm sorry that happened — that sounds frustrating. What went wrong specifically? Was it the leads weren't qualified, or they didn't show up, or the agency just stopped communicating? I want to understand so I can be honest about whether we'd actually be different or whether we'd hit the same wall."*

## Objection 9: Comparison ("How are you different from [Competitor]?")

### What it actually means

Surface: "How does this compare to [Other Vendor]?"

Real concern:
- "I'm shopping. I want to feel like I've made an informed decision."
- "I want to make sure I'm not missing something"

A comparison question is good news — they're considering you seriously. Don't bash competitors. That signals weakness.

### Why reps fumble it

- Bash the competitor ("[X] is overpriced and their service sucks")
- List feature differences without connecting to value
- Get into a feature war the prospect can't follow

### The pattern that works

```
Acknowledge:    "Good question. [Competitor] is solid — a lot of 
                 our clients evaluated them too."
Loop:           "What's drawing you to compare us? Like, what 
                 specific thing about [Competitor] is appealing? 
                 And what's giving you pause about them?"
Isolate:        Optional here. The loop usually reveals what matters.
Address:        Answer based on the loop. Don't run through every 
                difference — focus on the one or two things they 
                actually care about.
```

### Example rewrite

Bad rep response: *"We're way better than [X]. They have a lot of complaints online."*

Rewritten: *"Good question — [Competitor] is solid. A lot of our clients evaluate them too. What's drawing you toward them? Because there are situations where they're a great fit, and there are situations where we're a better fit, and it depends on what you're optimizing for."*

## Objection 10: Distrust of Sales ("I'm just looking around")

### What it actually means

Surface: "I'm just gathering information" or "I'm just shopping right now."

Real concern:
- "I'm in early-stage research mode and don't want to commit"
- "I've been burned by pushy sales reps and I'm protecting myself"
- "I don't trust myself to say no, so I'm pre-saying no"

### Why reps fumble it

- Accept it and end the call
- Push hard to "convert the lead" and confirm the prospect's worst fear
- Try to fake-disqualify ("Oh okay, well we're probably not for you anyway")

### The pattern that works

```
Acknowledge:    "Smart. Most people who buy are in research mode 
                 first."
Loop:           "What are you researching specifically? Because if 
                 I can answer questions, that's actually faster than 
                 reading a website."
Isolate:        Optional. The loop reveals their actual stage.
Address:        Be a resource. Don't push. Lower the pressure. 
                Most "just looking" prospects buy when they don't 
                feel sold to. Schedule a follow-up if the call ends 
                without a decision — but a real follow-up with a 
                date.
```

## Implicit objections (the ones nobody says)

The hardest objections to handle are the ones the prospect doesn't say. Watch for:

- **Long silence after price disclosure** — the price hit them and they're processing. Don't fill the silence — let them speak first.
- **"Interesting"** — neutral word, often hiding doubt. Probe: "What's making you say 'interesting'?"
- **Quick subject change after a key feature is mentioned** — they didn't connect with that feature. Bring it back.
- **Repetitive "okay... okay... okay"** — they've checked out. Pause and ask: "What's going through your head right now?"
- **The prospect starts taking calls or seeming distracted** — they're done. Either re-engage with a sharp question or schedule a real follow-up.

When the rep doesn't catch implicit objections, mark it in the report. These are usually the highest-leverage coaching moments.

## How to score objection handling

For each objection in the transcript, assess:

1. **Was it heard?** Did the rep notice the objection (especially implicit ones)?
2. **Was it acknowledged?** Did the rep validate before responding?
3. **Was it isolated?** Did the rep confirm this is the only blocker?
4. **Was it addressed?** Did the rep respond with evidence or just reassurance?
5. **Was momentum maintained?** Did the rep move the call forward after handling, or stall?

A perfect handling hits 5/5. A typical fumble misses 2-3. A complete miss skips the objection entirely.

## What to write in the rewritten response

When rewriting an objection response in the rep's voice:

1. **Preserve their voice** — if the rep says "yeah" instead of "yes," your rewrite says "yeah." If they don't use big words, neither does the rewrite.
2. **Match their energy** — don't make a low-energy rep suddenly sound assertive. Coach them up gradually.
3. **Use ALIA** — but make it feel natural, not formulaic.
4. **Keep it short** — most great objection responses are 2-3 sentences. Long responses feel rehearsed.
5. **Reference the actual call** — pull from what was said in discovery, not generic example data.

The rewrite should sound like the rep on a really good day — not like a different person.
