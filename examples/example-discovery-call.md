# Example: Discovery Call — Gym Owner (B- Grade)

This example shows a complete Sales Call Review run for a discovery call between a gym marketing agency rep and a gym owner. The call is decent but has clear coaching opportunities — perfect example of a B-grade discovery that's commonly seen.

## User input

```
Please review this sales call. It's a discovery call from yesterday.

Rep: Jordan (our setter — does discovery + qualification, hands off to closer)
Prospect: Brad Henderson, owner of Iron Works Fitness in Phoenix, AZ
Source: Filled out our website form 3 days ago
Offer context: We sell a 6-week challenge program for gyms — $1,500/month, 
  managed Facebook ads + funnel + lead nurture. Closer call follows discovery 
  if there's a fit.

Framework: We use NEPQ-style discovery, sort of — Jordan's been trained on 
  it but isn't religious about it.

Transcript from Fathom below:

---

[0:00] Jordan: Hey Brad, this is Jordan from GymGrowth. Can you hear me 
okay?

[0:04] Brad: Yeah, hey, can hear you fine.

[0:06] Jordan: Awesome. So I see you filled out our form a few days ago 
about getting some help with your gym marketing. Thanks for doing that. 
Before we dive in — how's your day going so far?

[0:18] Brad: It's alright, you know. Busy. Just got out of a class.

[0:23] Jordan: Cool, cool. Yeah I bet. So I just want to make sure we 
make this useful for you. Plan is — I'll ask you some questions to 
understand your situation, and if it makes sense, we'll book a follow-up 
call with one of our senior strategists who'll walk you through specifics. 
Sound good?

[0:42] Brad: Yeah, sounds good.

[0:43] Jordan: Cool. So tell me a little bit about Iron Works. How long 
you been open, what's the gym look like?

[0:51] Brad: Yeah so we've been open about four years now. We're a small 
gym, like 3,500 square feet. We do mostly group training — kind of 
HIIT-style stuff, some kettlebell work, that kind of thing. We have 
about, I don't know, maybe 110 active members right now.

[1:12] Jordan: Okay cool, 110 members. And what's been the biggest thing 
you're trying to solve right now?

[1:20] Brad: Honestly, just leads. We need more leads. We've kind of 
plateaued the last six months.

[1:27] Jordan: Yeah, plateau is tough. So you're saying leads — what's 
your current setup? Like how are you getting leads now?

[1:36] Brad: We're running some Facebook ads ourselves. My wife sort of 
runs them, she's been doing a course on it. We get some leads but they're 
honestly trash. Like, half of them don't even answer when we call.

[1:51] Jordan: Right, that's a common issue. So what kind of numbers 
are you seeing? Like what's your cost per lead, that kind of thing?

[2:01] Brad: Uh, I don't know exactly. We spend about $800 a month on 
ads. We get... I don't know, maybe 30 leads a month? Something like that.

[2:14] Jordan: Got it. And of those 30, how many become members?

[2:18] Brad: Honestly? Maybe one or two. It's pretty bad.

[2:24] Jordan: Yeah, that's rough. So you're spending $800 to get one 
or two members. That hurt to say out loud?

[2:31] Brad: [laughs] Yeah, kind of. I mean, I knew it was bad but 
hearing it that way, yeah.

[2:38] Jordan: I bet. So look, you said you've plateaued the last six 
months. What's a member worth to you in your gym? Like average length 
of stay times monthly fee?

[2:50] Brad: Our average member stays maybe 8 months. We charge $179 
a month. So I guess like $1,400 lifetime? Something like that.

[3:01] Jordan: Okay, $1,400 lifetime. And right now you're paying $400 
to acquire each member. So you're netting maybe a thousand bucks per 
member over their lifetime, but it's taking you forever to grow because 
you're only adding one or two a month.

[3:18] Brad: Yeah. I mean we get walk-ins too. But the ad stuff is just 
not working.

[3:25] Jordan: Got it. So besides the lead issue, anything else that 
you're trying to figure out?

[3:31] Brad: I mean, retention has been okay but we lose a few members 
every month. Wish that was better. And we don't really have any system 
for following up with people who try a class and don't sign up. They 
just... disappear.

[3:48] Jordan: Yeah, the trial-class follow-up is huge. Most gyms leave 
a ton of money on the table there. So you have leads coming in, but 
you're not closing them, and the ones who try a class and don't sign 
up just go away. Got it.

[4:05] Brad: Yeah pretty much.

[4:07] Jordan: Cool. Have you tried any agency or anything before?

[4:12] Brad: We tried one a couple years ago. It was a disaster. They 
took our money, ran some ads that didn't make sense for us, and we 
fired them after three months. Haven't really wanted to try again 
since.

[4:27] Jordan: Yeah, that's tough. What specifically went wrong with 
them?

[4:32] Brad: They just didn't seem to know our market. Like, they were 
running ads targeting people that aren't even in our demographic. We 
get older folks, mostly women in their 40s and 50s. They were running 
ads to like 22-year-olds.

[4:50] Jordan: That's brutal. Yeah, we work with a lot of gyms and 
demographic targeting is huge. Our typical client targets exactly 
that 40-60 demographic — we have specific ad copy for that audience.

[5:05] Brad: Yeah okay.

[5:08] Jordan: Cool. So tell me — you said you've been open four years. 
What's your goal? Where are you trying to take this?

[5:17] Brad: I want to get to like 200 members. That's where the gym 
becomes really profitable. Right now I'm taking home decent money but 
I'm working 60+ hours a week to do it. If I could hit 200 members, I 
could hire another coach and step back a bit.

[5:36] Jordan: Got it, so 200 is the magic number. And right now at 
110, you'd need to add 90 net new members to get there.

[5:45] Brad: Yeah.

[5:46] Jordan: Okay. So if nothing changes, where are you in 12 months?

[5:51] Brad: Probably about the same. Maybe 115 if I'm lucky. The 
plateau has been real.

[5:58] Jordan: That's tough. Twelve more months at 60 hours a week 
without breaking through.

[6:05] Brad: Yeah, that's not what I want.

[6:08] Jordan: Right. Okay so let me ask — you mentioned your wife 
running the ads. Is she the one who'd make the call on bringing in 
outside help, or is that you?

[6:18] Brad: That'd be me. She'd be involved but I'd make the call.

[6:23] Jordan: Got it. Okay, so based on what you've told me — gym 
that's plateaued, ads aren't working, no follow-up system on trial 
classes, goal is 200 members — I think we're a fit to talk further. 
What we do is run paid ads specifically for gyms targeting 40+ women 
demographics, with a 6-week challenge offer that converts well, plus 
we manage the lead nurture and the trial-class follow-up. Our typical 
client adds 20-40 members in their first 90 days.

[7:00] Brad: Yeah okay.

[7:02] Jordan: The next step is, I'd love to get you on a call with 
our senior strategist, Mackie. She'll walk you through the specifics, 
including the actual ad copy and the funnel and the pricing. That call 
is usually about 45 minutes. Do you have a time later this week or 
early next week that works?

[7:25] Brad: Yeah I think Thursday afternoon could work. Like 2pm or 
3pm?

[7:31] Jordan: Thursday at 2pm works perfect. I'll send you a calendar 
invite right after we hang up. Mackie will be ready to dig into the 
specifics with you.

[7:42] Brad: Cool. What about the price thing? Just so I'm not 
surprised.

[7:48] Jordan: Yeah good question. Mackie will walk you through the 
full pricing on the call but generally we're looking at investments 
in the $1,500-$2,000 a month range, plus your ad spend. She'll go 
through it in detail with you so you have full clarity.

[8:08] Brad: Okay, that's a lot. But yeah, Thursday at 2 works.

[8:14] Jordan: Awesome. Calendar invite incoming. Anything else you 
want to make sure we cover with Mackie on Thursday?

[8:22] Brad: I mean, I want to know how it'd actually work. Like the 
mechanics. And how soon we'd see results.

[8:29] Jordan: Perfect. I'll let her know to focus on those areas. 
Talk to you Thursday, Brad.

[8:34] Brad: Sounds good. Thanks Jordan.

[8:36] Jordan: Bye now.

---
End of transcript. About 8.5 minutes total.
```

PRIORITY 1: Add the consequence layer to discovery
   Impact: HUGE. Currently your discovery quantifies pain (the math)

   single highest-leverage thing you can add to your discovery is 
   one consequence question per pain point, and the discipline to

   Impact: HIGH. You did the closer's light job at 6:23 and 7:48.

ROLE-PLAY SCRIPT — NEXT 1:1

DRILL: Discovery Deepening — Specifically the Consequence Layer

Length: 5 minutes
Setup: Manager plays a gym owner who's plateaued. Use Brad's actual 
profile — Iron Works Fitness, 110 members, 4 years open, 60-hour 
work weeks, goal of 200 members. Manager-Brad will give cooperative 
discovery answers but flat consequence answers.

Manager opening: "Yeah we've been stuck around 110 for the last 6

Rep's job:
1. Get to the same place you got to in the real call (the $800/month, 
   1-2 members, $1,400 LTV math) within 3 minutes — fast quantification
2. Then in the next 2 minutes, drill consequence questions specifically:
   - "If nothing changes in 12 months, where are you?"
   - [Wait for answer] "And what does that mean for you personally?"
   - [Wait for answer] "Walk me through what end-of-year 2026 looks 
     like if you're still at 110 members and still working 60-hour 
     weeks."
3. Sit in each answer for 3+ seconds before responding
4. Resist the urge to rescue with "but we can fix that"

Success criteria:
- Rep gets at least one moment where Brad articulates emotional 
  consequence (frustration, exhaustion, fear of staying stuck)
- Rep doesn't pitch
- Rep doesn't rescue
- After 5 minutes, manager can name what would change in Brad's life 
  if his gym DID hit 200 members — meaning rep got that out of him

Variations:
- Easier: Manager-Brad articulates consequences without much pulling
- Harder: Manager-Brad gives flat answers ("yeah, just stuck") that 
  require rep to ask 2-3 follow-up questions to surface the real 
  consequence
- Hardest: Manager-Brad deflects to logistics ("yeah but how would 
  this work") and rep has to redirect back to consequences before 
  pitching
